The Subjective Eye

Embryo selection is the single most impactful decision in IVF — yet it's done by eye, and embryologists disagree 50% of the time on borderline cases.

RUNNING EXAMPLE Embryo #47 — Day 5 Blastocyst Patient: 34yo, AMH 2.1 ng/mL, BMI 24.2

Embryo #47 is a Day-5 blastocyst from a 34-year-old patient in Israel. When shown to the panel, it received IPG grades ranging from 2 to 4 across 36 embryologists — a "fair quality" embryo where human agreement was lowest (κ = 0.25). Eleven embryologists said "don't transfer," while 25 said "transfer." The actual outcome: successful implantation and fetal heartbeat at 7 weeks. The majority got lucky, but their reasoning was inconsistent.

THE CORE CHALLENGE

No embryologist characteristic — years of experience, country, degree, or grading system — significantly predicted accuracy. The subjectivity is inherent in visual assessment itself.

— Fordham et al., Human Reproduction, 2022
WHAT THE DATA SHOWS

A panel of 36 embryologists from 13 countries assessed 136 blastocyst time-lapse videos with known implantation outcomes. Each embryo received an implantation probability grade from 1 (very low) to 5 (very high).

Embryologists showed good agreement on clearly good or poor embryos (κ = 0.65), but only fair agreement on borderline “fair quality” embryos (κ = 0.25). This is exactly the cohort where selection matters most — and where AI can add the greatest value.
